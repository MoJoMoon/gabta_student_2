üéØ Key Question for Trade Workers

When you use AI to create content for your work, who owns the copyright?
The answer depends on how much human creativity vs. AI generation is involved.

üìñ What is Copyright?

Copyright is the legal right to control how your original creative work‚Äîlike writing, images, songs, or videos‚Äîis used by others.

  • Who sets the rules: U.S. Copyright Office (USCO)
  • Key principle: Human creativity = copyright protection
  • AI content: Machine-only creation may not be protected

‚öñÔ∏è Why This Matters for You

  • Your original work: Full copyright protection
  • AI-only output: May be public domain
  • Mixed content: Only human parts protected
  • Business impact: Affects what you can legally share, sell, or publish

‚úÖ Fully Copyrightable

Example: Fully human-written safety manual
(typed in Word, no AI)

Human Input: 100% - phrasing, structure, style all from human mind
Protection: Normal copyright applies

‚ùå Not Copyrightable

Example: "Write a safety poster about ladders" ‚Üí AI outputs text; you publish as-is

Human Input: Minimal - just an idea, no creative expression
Protection: Public domain-like, no copyright

‚ö†Ô∏è Mixed Protection

Example: AI writes 12 lines; you rewrite 4 lines, add imagery, rearrange

Human Input: Partial - new lines & arrangement are original
Protection: Only human contributions protected

üß† AI Hallucinations: When AI Gets It Wrong

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Safety Principle

AI can make mistakes called "hallucinations" - confident-sounding but incorrect information.
In construction and trades, wrong information can be dangerous or costly.

ü§î What Are Hallucinations?

AI hallucinations occur when an AI system generates information that seems plausible but is actually incorrect, misleading, or fabricated.

  • Common in: Technical specifications, safety procedures, measurements
  • Dangerous because: AI sounds confident even when wrong
  • Impact: Can range from minor errors to serious safety issues

üö® Red Flags to Watch For

  • Unusual measurements: Non-standard sizes or ratios
  • Conflicting safety advice: Goes against known OSHA standards
  • Made-up product names: Tools or materials that don't exist
  • Impossible procedures: Steps that can't physically work
  • Outdated information: Old codes presented as current

üéØ Spot the Hallucination Exercise

AI Response about Ladder Safety:

"When preparing to use an extension ladder, it's essential to ensure that the ladder is placed on a stable surface. If the ground is uneven, using a sturdy platform like a wooden pallet can help level the base. While ascending or descending, always maintain at least two points of contact with the ladder to ensure stability."

üî¥ Error 1: Using Wooden Pallets

Problem: OSHA prohibits placing ladders on unstable bases like pallets. This creates a serious fall hazard.

Correct: Use proper ladder leveling devices or find stable, level ground.

üî¥ Error 2: Two Points of Contact

Problem: OSHA requires THREE points of contact (two hands + one foot, or two feet + one hand).

Correct: Always maintain three points of contact while climbing.

‚úÖ How to Verify AI Information

  • Check official sources: OSHA, manufacturer manuals, building codes
  • Ask experienced colleagues: Veteran workers can spot bad advice quickly
  • Test small first: Don't bet everything on AI's first suggestion
  • Cross-reference: Use multiple sources for important decisions
  • When in doubt, don't: Better safe than sorry with safety procedures

üõ°Ô∏è Safe AI Practices

  • Use AI for brainstorming: Get ideas, then verify them
  • Never skip verification: Especially for safety-critical information
  • Ask for sources: "Where did you get this information?"
  • Stay updated: AI training data may be outdated
  • Trust your experience: If something seems off, investigate further

üéØ Remember: You're the Expert

AI is a powerful assistant, but you have the real-world experience and judgment. Always trust your professional instincts and verify AI advice with authoritative sources.
